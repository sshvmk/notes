### What is fractional reserve banking ?
It is a system where only a fraction of bank deposits are backed by actual cash on hand and available for withdrawals. In other words, banks only keep a specific amount of cash at hand and the remaining deposited cash is used to fund loans. This system is used by most commercial banks worldwide. 

This system allows banks to create money through lending while also maintaining sufficient reserves to meet day to day customer withdrawal demands that may arise. 

### How it works ? 
The reserve requirement is set by central banks and represents the minimum amount of customer deposits a bank must hold as reserves. 
Say, the reserve requirement is *10%* and you deposit *\$1,000* dollars in your bank account. This means the bank must keep at least *\$100* as reserve. The remaining *\$900* can be lent out to customers. 
This mechanism of keeping only a "fraction" of deposited money and giving rest of the deposit as loan, creates new money in economy. These loans then get deposited elsewhere, creating a multiplier effect. 

In some countries, reserve requirements have been replaced by capital requirements or other regulations.

### Money Multiplier Effect
One of the most fascinating aspects of fractional reserve banking is how it enables money creation through the banking system:

- Initial deposit: You deposit $1,000 in Bank A 
- Bank A keeps \$100 (10% reserve) and lends \$900 to Person B 
- Person B deposits $900 in Bank B 
- Bank B keeps \$90 (10% reserve) and lends \$810 to Person C \
And so on...

Through this process, the original \$1,000 deposit can theoretically create up to \$10,000 in new money in the economy (when the reserve requirement is 10%).
The maximum money creation potential can be calculated using:
$$
MoneyMultiplier = \frac {1}{ReserveRatio}
$$ 

### How Fractional Reserve Banking Affects the Economy
1. **Economic Growth**
    - Enables greater lending than would otherwise be possible
    - Facilitates business expansion, home purchases, and education funding
    - Increases money velocity throughout the economy
2. **Monetary Policy**
    - Central banks can influence money supply by adjusting reserve requirements
    - Changes in interest rates affect lending behavior
    - Open market operations impact bank reserves
3. **Financial Stability**
    - Requires careful balance between lending and maintaining sufficient reserves
    - Too little reserves: increased vulnerability to bank runs
    - Too much reserves: reduced economic activity from limited lending

### Criticisms of Fractional Reserve Banking
1. **Bank Runs** - When depositors lose confidence and attempt to withdraw funds simultaneously, banks may face a liquidity crisis since they don't have enough cash on hand to cover all deposits. \
    > This can be witnessed by reviewing the Greek financial crisis that began in 2009. In 2015, Greece defaulted on its debts to the International Monetary Fund amidst a global financial crisis. As a result, citizens flocked to the banks to withdraw their funds, and the banks were forced to close their doors to prevent a complete withdrawal of capital from a struggling system.

    > The 2008 Financial Crisis, banks lent out large amounts of money, often with inadequate reserves, leading to a collapse in the housing market. Governments and central banks stepped in to provide liquidity and prevent a complete collapse of the financial system.

2. **Systemic Risk** - A failure in one part of the banking system can cascade throughout the economy, as witnessed during the 2008 financial crisis.
3. **Economic Cycles** - Some economists argue that fractional reserve banking amplifies economic cycles, creating excessive credit during booms and severe contractions during busts.
4. **Economic Overheat** - When the economy is expanding, it is growing. Consumers tend to spend more, and banks lend more during periods of expansion. When more money is being created through loans, demand can soar, increasing prices. Producers begin producing more to meet demand. This can continue into a situation where the economy overheats, growing too fast.
